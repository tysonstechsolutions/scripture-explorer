# The World Before Israel

## Setting the Stage

Before there was Israel, before Abraham left his homeland, before Moses stood before Pharaoh—there was a world already ancient, already complex, already wrestling with the same questions we ask today.

{image:mesopotamia-map}

## The Cradle of Civilization

Around 3500 BCE, in the fertile land between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers—a region the Greeks would later call "Mesopotamia" (meaning "between the rivers")—something remarkable was happening. The Sumerians were building cities, inventing writing, and creating the first complex civilization we know of.

{image:cuneiform-tablet}

This matters for our story because this is where it all begins. When Genesis opens with "In the beginning," it's speaking to people who already had creation stories. The Babylonians had the *Enuma Elish*, where the world is formed from the corpse of a slain goddess. The Egyptians had multiple creation myths involving gods emerging from primordial waters.

{scripture:Genesis 1:1-2}
> In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth. Now the earth was formless and empty, darkness was over the surface of the deep, and the Spirit of God was hovering over the waters.

The Hebrew account is doing something radical here: instead of gods battling for supremacy, there's one God who simply speaks, and it is. No conflict. No divine corpses. Just creative word.

## Abraham's World

{image:ziggurat-ur}

When Abraham (then called Abram) receives his call to leave Ur, he's not leaving some backwater village. Ur was a sophisticated city of perhaps 65,000 people—massive for its time. It had libraries, schools, a complex legal system, and a towering ziggurat dedicated to the moon god Nanna.

{image:standard-of-ur}

{scripture:Genesis 12:1-3}
> The Lord had said to Abram, "Go from your country, your people and your father's household to the land I will show you. I will make you into a great nation, and I will bless you; I will make your name great, and you will be a blessing. I will bless those who bless you, and whoever curses you I will curse; and all peoples on earth will be blessed through you."

Think about what this meant. Leave everything—family, culture, security, the known world—for a promise from a God his society didn't worship, to go to a land he'd never seen.

## Egypt: The Superpower

{image:pyramids-giza}

To the southwest lay Egypt, already over a thousand years into its dynastic period by Abraham's time. The pyramids at Giza were already ancient. Egypt would loom large in Israel's story—as refuge, as oppressor, as temptation, as symbol.

When famine drives Abraham's descendants to Egypt {scripture:Genesis 46:1-7}, they're entering the most powerful, most stable civilization of the ancient world. Four hundred years later, they'll leave as a nation—but that's getting ahead of ourselves.

## The Law Before Moses

{image:code-hammurabi}

Long before Moses received the Torah on Mount Sinai, the ancient world had law codes. The Code of Hammurabi, written around 1750 BCE, contained 282 laws covering everything from property rights to medical malpractice to marriage contracts.

When we read the laws in Exodus and Leviticus, we can see both similarities and striking differences with these older codes. The Torah doesn't emerge in a vacuum—it's speaking into an existing conversation about justice, society, and the divine order.

{deep-dive:ancient-law-codes}

## The Land Between

The land that would become Israel sat at a crossroads—literally. It was the land bridge connecting Africa to Asia, the path every army, every trade caravan, every migration had to cross. Egypt to the south, Mesopotamian powers to the north and east.

This geography is destiny. Israel would never be left alone. It would always be caught between empires, always struggling for independence, always being invaded, conquered, or threatened.

{map:ancient-near-east-trade-routes}

This precarious position shaped everything—their theology, their politics, their understanding of God's protection and judgment. When prophets warned about trusting in foreign alliances instead of God, this is what they meant.

## What Comes Next

We've set the stage. The world into which Abraham steps is not empty—it's full of gods, empires, and ancient wisdom. The story that unfolds will constantly interact with this world, sometimes adopting its forms, sometimes radically subverting them.
